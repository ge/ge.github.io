Howdy, again, George & Iris.

Great news about the room! In the nick of time.

You probably have it nailed, with your laptop (iPad?), GPS in the car (?), etc., but ...

From 101, take exit 731; turn right at the stop sign (Seawood Drive?), drive a short distance, and then turn left onto Patricks point Drive. Trinidad Inn is a bit more than a mile South, on the right.

From The Inn, it's about a mile or a bit more to Main Street, Trinidad. There is a food market near the intersection, in case you need stuff to make dinner at The Inn.

If you cross Main Street, Patrick's Point Drive becomes Scenic Drive, which is where the casino restaurant is located.

If you turn right onto Main Street (from Patrick's Point) you will likely end up at the harbor. Main runs into Trinity Street, which ends at Edwards Street. You will see Trinidad Bay and Head. There is a lighthouse memorial mini-park there; a memorial to those "lost at sea". Following Edwards Street will take you past the marine lab and down to the harbor. Be sure to check out the great open-ocean beach (on the right) before the drive to the harbor parking lot.

Trinidad Head and the nearby ocean beach are great places to catch the sunset (fog permitting). As are the several lookout points at or near Patrick's Point State Park, which will be 3 — 4 miles North of The Inn.

Whenever you have a good view of the ocean, look for whales. It is not "official" whale season (March - June, heading North), but we've seen more whales in August than we have "in season". We've usually seen solitary cow/calf pairs, with no sign of other whales. Often, they just chug along near the surface, barely submerged. They hug the coastline to avoid their chief predator, the Great Whites.

Howdy, George & Iris!

George, regarding our phone conversation last night, I, too, was/am confused. I had forgotten how far South Medford is from Portland. I guess I was thinking of Corvallis or Eugene. So going to Florence isn't going to be practical.

It looks like the best way to Highway 101 from Medford would be to take Highway US 199 from Grants Pass, OR to Crescent City, CA. According to Google, that route is 111 miles (2 hours, 9 minutes) from Medford. Mary likely knows a lot about that drive.

From Crescent City to Trinidad (US 101), CA is 62 miles (1 hour, 9 minutes), per Google.

So... the total drive from Medford to Trinidad should be about 173 miles and 3 hours, 18 minutes. You should have enough time to check-in to your lodging and enjoy a leisurely romp around Trinidad Harbor. Reserve your lodging ASAP; this is the height of tourist season, and there are not that many "good" places to stay in the area.

The drive from Trinidad to Cloverdale is 208 miles (3 hours, 34 minutes). This drive-time may not be realistic; I recall needing about 4 to 5 hours for this drive, but we enjoy frequent photo-opportunity stops.

Anyway, it looks like about a total of 381 miles from Medford to Cloverdale (Which you've noted in your schedule.), probably about a 7 hour drive, not counting stops. So... Trinidad is nearly the half-way point between Medford and Cloverdale. I recommend a layover in Trinidad. IMHO, there is no place even remotely as nice for a layover as Trinidad, between there and Cloverdale.

Trinidad's signature feature is a beautiful small, sheltered harbor and wharf, with a nice beach on the ocean side. Humboldt State University has a marine lab there and maintains a research vessel in the harbor. Many decades ago, I lived in a small house next to the marine lab for a few months, while house and job sitting for a friend who was a sort-of manager/caretaker of the lab.

The place we usually stay in the Trinidad area is the Trinidad Inn; 1170 Patricks Point Drive, Trinidad, CA 95570; 707-677-3349     www.trinidadinn.com/
Take Patrick’s Point exit, North of Trinidad. The motel is on the West side (right) of Patrick's Point Drive, heading South.

This place is nothing fancy, but kinda cute, clean, with pleasant grounds and surroundings. Last time here (2007), we stayed in room 4, which had a bedroom (queen bed) and a full kitchen, for about $90/night. I'm sure it's more, now. The pictures on the Web site are accurate.

The last time we stayed in Trinidad (2008), the entire Trinidad Inn was booked up for large wedding party, so we ended up staying at View Crest Lodge; 3415 Patrick’s Point Drive; Trinidad, CA. This place is on the East side of Patrick's Point Drive, and has a series of individual cabins along a winding hillside driveway. They only had one cabin left, which was very small, but cute and comfortable, and with a partial ocean view (And we could hear the sea lions barking.). It had a queen bed, and a small refrigerator and a microwave oven, which are both a near-necessity for Xandra. We paid $95/night; room 1A.

We haven't been to Trinidad since 2008, but the good dining choices have always been very limited. When we've experimented, we've been disappointed. Our usuals have been the Trinidad Bay Eatery & Gallery, 607 Parker St, Trinidad, CA 95570 (for breakfast and/or lunch), and the Sunset Restaurant at the "Cher-Ae-Heights" Casino; 27 Scenic Drive, Trinidad (dinner).

Things change, so no guarantees, but we've had good breakfasts at the Trinidad Bay Eatery. Another good breakfast/lunch place is Beachcomber Cafe; 363 Trinity Street. For some reason it was closed the last few times we were in Trinidad. Both of these places can get very busy.

There is an old restaurant right down in the harbor, the Seascape, which was a great, if funky, "locals" spot for breakfast, and for clam chowder & fish 'n' chips. The casino tribe has taken it over and it looked very busy at lunchtime, the last time we were down at the harbor. I haven't eaten there for a very long time, so I don't know how it is.

We've eaten dinner at the Sunset Restaurant (at casino) about six times. Each time we've been very pleased. Usually we get a window view table; there is a beautiful view of Trinidad Head and Harbor and of the open ocean.
The food has always been very good, but not inexpensive. Xandra usually orders a steak, which (with salad, potato, etc.) is about $35. I usually order several appetizers (clam chowder, crab cakes, salad) instead of an entrée.
The clam chowder, and the crab cakes, were among the best I've ever eaten. If I remember correctly, you can walk in the casino front entrance and (to the right) almost directly into the restaurant area, without having to walk past all of the "gaming" devices and the pathetic losers who haven't learned that casinos aren't built to give money away.

Gualala

Gualala! We're hoping we four can spend a few days together at our old coastal getaway lodging, "Breakers Inn". We stayed there for two nights, several weeks ago (May 16 & 17). We hadn't been there for several years, but have been visiting for about 12 years, usually twice a year. Now that we don't have Rebecca's family living with us, the "need to flee" is not imperative.

You may recall, California has severely restricted coastal development (thankfully!). This is one of the very few coastal lodgings in NorCal that is right on the water, at the mouth of the Gualala River. The rooms face the ocean and most have outdoor balconies. The Gualala river is about 60 feet from the balconies, and the ocean is about 150 yards away. The two bodies of water are separated by a sandbar. The sandbar is part of the Gualala Point Regional Park (Sonoma County). The park and long sandbar beach would be great for hiking (We've not tried that, yet.).

If you didn't know, the Gualala River forms the coastal boundary between Sonoma and Mendocino Counties. The rooms at Breakers Inn are named after U.S. states, or other countries. Our favorite has always been "Pennsylvania", where we stayed once again. Gualala is in a coastal "banana belt" and has weather that is usually much more pleasant than, say, Bodega Bay.

Here are a few links, so that you'll have an idea of what I'm enthused about. In the link below, you can get a glimpse of our view in the photo (s) of our favorite room.
