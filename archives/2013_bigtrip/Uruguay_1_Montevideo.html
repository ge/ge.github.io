Montevideo

Tues Oct 15

We arrived in Montevideo fairly early in the morning and took a bus to the Hotel Esplendor, an upscale hotel in a business district where there were few residences.

The hotel had very poor Wi-Fi: we had to go to one part of the lobby or breakfast room to connect.

The top floor, which had a very nice view, was used for public events like breakfast. Letting all hotel patrons use the top floor is like having laws that don't allow building between the ocean and the first public road, so that everyone could enjoy access to water.

Montevideo was a very nice city, relatively clean and bright, buildings not too tall. It had an amazing bookstore, reminiscent of the now-defunct Scribner's bookstore in New York City.


We took a long bus tour, which was not very enjoyable but which did give us a reasonable overview of the city.



We hung out in the lobby for awhile using their Wi-Fi, which didn't work in the rooms. George practiced his French with a dancer from a Belgian dance company that was sitting in a circle. Then we walked a few blocks to a main street, where we found Il Mondo della Pizza, a pizza place with a large area set back from the sidewalk, where people could sit in the outside air to drink beer and eat pizza and watch Uruguay play Argentina. It was a very exciting match. We watched the whole thing, enjoying cheering with the crowd as Uruguay won. It was a one-pizza, three-bottle night of fun.

Weds, Oct 16

We had breakfast in the wonderful top floor restaurant, which had a view of the city and the Rio de la Plata, which separates Uruguay from Argentina and looks like an ocean. It reminded George of the beautiful hotel in Sfax, Tunisia, where he and Iris stayed a few years ago.

George spoke with another dancer, who he ran into in the stairs to the restaurant. It turned out that the company, [Peeping Tom}(http://www.peepingtom.be) were going to perform that evening in the Theatro Solis, a beautiful old theater just off the main square in Montevideo. So we bought tickets during the day.

We walked to the port to buy tickets to Buenos Aires on the Buquebus (hydroplane). We wandered around the port area, visiting a Carnaval museum that contained this weird piece.


We walked into a large public market and discovered that there were restaurant tables scattered among the food vendors, a pattern that we found on the rest of our trip. It makes for lively and fun places to eat.





The ice bucket for our beer was a classy touch.





Having hightly approved of the food, David soon found himself with a new job!





We walked along Montevideo's beautiful river coast.




This was our last evening in Montevideo, so we went to the Teatro Solis to see Peeping Tom perform 32 Rue Vandenbranden, a deeply wierd dance piece worthy of the American Dance Festival. Then we had dinner in our favorite pizza place, only this time inside.

Montevideo to Colonia (Thurs, Oct 17)

Bus Trip

We started the morning with an early breakfast in our hotel. As George walked out of the breakfast room, he ran into the older woman dancer, the one with the wild hair who sang during the performance. David came out shortly thereafter. George told her (in French) how much they had liked the dance. She said that it had been very hard to stage the performance (the logistics were daunting) and said that there had been mistakes. She said that she had pounded on the secret door (in the set) and was relieved that we hadn't noticed that she had broken it! The company is based on improvisation and is not choreographed by any one person.

We went to a hardware store where David bought a good metal thermos for his chimarrão; this replaced the cheap, red plastic thermos he had bought in Brazil.

We took a city bus to the bus station, where we took the bus to Colonia.

We passed through flat agricultural country, sighting one huge wind generator. It was fascinating.
