Buenos Aires

As soon as we got into port, David asked for get a refund for the difference in price between the boat trip we paid for (a hydroplane) and the one we got (a plodder). He got back about $20 in Argentinian pesos.

David had arranged for an apartment in Buenos Aires over the internet, so we went straight there from the port. We were let in by Clayton, a Brazilian. What a relief to find someone who spoke Portuguese.

We took our laundry, which had been accumulating for awhile, to a laundromat and left it and walked to an old market and where we found a little coffee bar named Coffee Town. David had a long talk with the woman working there about the many varieties of coffee in the world.



We walked to a slightly mysterious place where Clayton had told us we could exchange the dollars in our moneybelts for Argentinian pesos and wouldn't get taken in by bad bills. On the way we passed through the main square, the Plaza de Mayo, where people were still protesting the abuses of the past military dictatorship.


We took a tiny elevator to the top floor, the 14th, and knocked on a door labeled "imobiliaria" The two rather apprenhensive men who opened the door asked who sent us. Our answer satisfied them and we soon got almost double the official exchange rate!

We walked a bit and then sat in the Plaza San Martin for awhile before taking a long walk to a huge riverside park, the Reserva Ecológica Costanera Sur where we took a 5km walk. As we emerged from the park, David stopped at a little grill where he ordered chorípan and beer. The chorípan was wonderful: we searched for it throughout the rest of the trip, rarely finding any as good as the chorípan we found in Buenos Aires.






We walked back to our apartment, where George took a long nap. After that we worked on plans for the rest of the trip.

Sat, Oct 19

Breakfast at CoffeeTown again. This time we drank Chemex coffee made with Guatemalan coffee and ate half-moons with ham and cheese, followed by tiny French-style apple tarts.

Then we bought some cheese and sliced salami, an amazingly difficult task in a language we can't understand in a system we don't understand.

The laundry wasn't ready yet so we went back to the apartment.

After awhile David went out for the laundry and a bus card and a bus guide that we used to take a bus to the immense Recoleta Cemetery, where we spend a couple of hours.








After we left the cemetery, we ate some delicious fruit cups from a stand and walked for a very long way on what turned out to be the wrong street. We finally realized we had passed the art museum we were looking for and that it was a long walk back to it. We went to a nearby Japanese Tea Garden instead, where we discovered that it was cosplay day! People were dressed as animé characters.







Again we walked a lot and finally found a stand that made bondiola sandwiches, something that David had been searching for - it wasn't too good, but we did get to feed some of it to parrots!





We took a taxi to an art museum of contemporary art. David and George passed this painting at separate times and both us us noticed how people seemed to fixate on it, standing silently while staring at it.


We took a bus back to the apartment, from where we walked to a nearby square filled with tables. We sat down and drank beer and ate peanuts. Altogether a very pleasant evening.

Sun, Oct 20

It rained a lot last night and skies are overcast and it is expected to rain more.

We are being wonderfully lazy this morning.

Around midday, we took a long bus ride to a suburb known for its connection to gaucho culture, expecting to find a fair with music. We got off the bus only to discover that the fair was empty. We talked with the owner of an actual gaucho horse gear shop who told us that the fair had been canceled because of the weather, even though the rain had stopped by this time.

We decided to eat lunch before we headed back down town. While David waited to be seated in a sidewalk restaurant, George found a dark passageway promising to lead to a restaurant. A woman there told George it had good food and was cheap.

George went back and got David, who loved the adventure. We went in and sat at a picnic table and had sandwiches and beer. Musicians were rehearsing in a corner, playing fabulous music typical of the region. When they got on stage and played, people began to dance traditional dances. It was extraordinary.














The toilet there reminded George of the Peace Corps.


This a street fair on the street where we lived. It was interesting, especially the older, artistic-looking people that seemed especially common in Buenos Aires. David looked for a belt to go with his father's buckle, but never found one. He did find some CDs of tango music that we later played in our rental car in Chile.







Our landlords, Virginie and Clayton, visited for a while but couldn't stay for dinner. David and George ate at El Florida, sitting at the bar. It was mediocre, but the beer was good.
